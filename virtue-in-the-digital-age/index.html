Virtue in the Digital Age

Aristotle never had a Twitter account

October 2025 · 6 min read

The virtues were forged for the agora, not the algorithm. Aristotle's account of human excellence presupposed a world of face-to-face encounters, where reputation was built over a lifetime and character was legible in the habits of daily life. Courage meant standing firm in battle. Temperance meant moderation at the feast. Justice meant giving each citizen their due. These concepts, refined over millennia, now collide with a world their architects could not have imagined.

What does courage look like when the battlefield is a comment section? What does temperance mean when the feast never ends — when content is infinite and the scroll is bottomless? What does justice require when an algorithm decides who is heard and who is silenced? These are not rhetorical questions. They are the urgent moral puzzles of our time.

We have built machines that optimize for engagement, and engagement, it turns out, is the enemy of nearly every classical virtue.

Consider the Socratic commitment to self-examination. The examined life requires stillness, patience, and the willingness to sit with uncertainty. Social media rewards the opposite: speed, certainty, and the performance of conviction. The person who pauses to think before posting is algorithmically punished. The person who reacts instantly and emotionally is rewarded with reach.

This creates what we might call a virtue trap: the very qualities that make someone morally admirable — nuance, humility, openness to being wrong — are precisely the qualities that reduce their influence in digital spaces. Meanwhile, the vices — dogmatism, outrage, tribal loyalty — are amplified. As questions of moral luck remind us, the moral quality of a digital life may depend less on character than on the particular algorithmic weather one encounters.

The question of governance looms large here. The social contract must now extend to the platforms that mediate so much of civic life. When a private company's content moderation decisions affect public discourse more than any government censor, the old distinctions between public and private, state and market, become dangerously inadequate.

And yet, I remain cautiously hopeful. The virtues have survived the fall of Athens, the collapse of Rome, the upheavals of modernity. They may yet survive the algorithm. But only if we are willing to practice the ancient art of refusal — to say no to the machine's demands and yes to the slower, harder, more human work of becoming good.
