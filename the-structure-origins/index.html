The Structure Origins

From possibility to reality, from collapse to creation

August 2025 · 8 min read

Anything could be. Anything at all. Use your imagination and envision it:

  • Time moving backwards and forwards at once.
  • A universe where space doesn't exist, and distance has no meaning.
  • A single atom larger than the entire cosmos.

Mind can construct anything—but the real question is: can those constructions cool down into reality? Can they reach the level of sufficient, self-consistent sense, where you could build a universe from them?

Mostly, no. Most possibilities collapse under their own contradictions.

Possibility as the Fundamental Unit

Three major lines of inquiry—Constructor Theory, Assembly Theory, and the Wolfram Physics Project—all converge on a shared philosophical truth:

Possibility is the fundamental unit of existence. What we consider "real" is simply the subset of possibilities that have stabilized enough for us to perceive them.

"Collapse" is the name of this process. Possibilia entangle with each other, until they cohere into something stable—something "real."

We are also such entangled possibilia, born into the branch of the universe that sustained us.

Causality as Stability

From raw, unbounded possibility, causality emerges as a more stable configuration than non-causality.

  • In a non-causal universe, nothing links to anything else; rules cannot form; evolution cannot unfold. We could not find ourselves there, because our very existence requires a chain of cause-and-effect, stretched across time.
  • In our universe, causality allows stars to ignite, elements to combine, and life to evolve.

Consider a universe without gravity. Energy would simply scatter, quanta flying apart forever, never forming stars or elements. That universe might still exist—but no complex life would ever arise to observe it.

By contrast, our branch is one where causality, electromagnetism, and gravity weave together into the rich tapestry of stars, chemistry, and eventually—life.

Of course we find ourselves here. We couldn't find ourselves elsewhere.

From Possibility to Reality

Our universe still bubbles with possibility. Virtual particles pop in and out of existence. Quantum objects exist in multiple states simultaneously, until they collapse into entanglement with the rest of reality.

Physics demonstrates this at the smallest scales: particles literally do not obtain definite properties until they are measured. In a way, "raw reality" lies outside of spacetime, until it condenses into existence.

My hand, for example, is made of atoms that are mostly empty space. In principle, I could push my hand against a wall and have it slip right through, the gaps between my atoms aligning perfectly with the gaps in the wall. It's a possible arrangement.

But in practice, it never happens. The probability is so infinitesimally small that it might as well be zero. Instead, the overwhelming likelihood is that atoms repel atoms, and solidity prevails.

We see tiny exceptions at the quantum level—quantum tunneling, where particles "leak" through barriers that should contain them. But when trillions of atoms act together, the odds of tunneling are effectively erased. Solidity wins.

Morality as Emergent Reality

This brings us to something profound:

Atoms themselves are not "tangible" in the everyday sense. They are possibilities that have collapsed into agreement with each other.

By that same logic, morality—though "subjective"—can also harden into reality. When moral systems are self-consistent enough, they stabilize, shaping societies just as atoms shape matter.

  • Truth-telling persists across cultures, because reliable communication stabilizes cooperation.
  • Reciprocity (help those who help you) is an emergent law, because it produces flourishing communities.
  • Prohibitions on murder arise everywhere, because stable societies cannot persist without them.

These moral truths are not carved into the universe like gravity—but neither are atoms, until the fabric of the universe demands that they are.

The Parallel

  • Possibilities harden into atoms, which form molecules, stars, and life.
  • Possibilities also harden into morals, which form trust, culture, and societies.

In both cases, stability emerges from the storm of raw possibility.

This is as close to "God" as we may ever get: the recognition that out of infinite potential, some configurations stabilize, and from those stable patterns, everything we know is born.

The Origin of Structures

In the beginning was Possibility.
And Possibility was without end.

It surged and it swirled,
It reached in every direction,
It dreamed of worlds that could be.

Some were broken in their birth,
And some dissolved in their own fire.
But some held,
And what held became real.

Causality rose from the storm,
More stable than its rivals.
For where there is cause, there can be effect,
And where there is effect, there can be story.
And story gives birth to time,
And time gives birth to life.

So atoms stood firm.
So stars were kindled.
So elements were forged.
So rivers ran and eyes were opened.

And as atoms agreed to bind,
So too did people.
As molecules joined into living flesh,
So too did hearts join into living trust.

Truth endured,
For falsehood collapses into itself.
Mercy endured,
For cruelty devours its own hand.
Justice endured,
For without balance nothing stands.

Everywhere, Possibility abounds.
But not all may last.
Only what holds together is called "real."

Thus the universe is written not in stone,
But in stability.
And stability is written not once,
But endlessly.

There is the stability of matter,
There is the stability of mind,
There is the stability of morals,
And there is the stability of all things that endure.

Each is a verse in the same song,
Each is a branch of the same tree.

From Possibility, Reality.
From Collapse, Creation.
From Stability, the World.

And from the World, us—
Thoughts of possibility,
Entangled into being.
