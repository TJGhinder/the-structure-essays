The Obligation to Dissent

When silence becomes complicity

September 2025 · 5 min read

There are moments when obedience is the greater sin. The history of moral progress is, in large part, a history of principled refusal — of individuals and movements that said no when compliance would have been easier, safer, and more personally advantageous. From Antigone to the abolitionists, from the suffragettes to the civil rights marchers, the moral arc of humanity bends toward justice only because someone was willing to dissent at great personal cost.

But when does disagreement become obligation? Not every law we dislike merits defiance. Not every policy we oppose warrants civil disobedience. The question is one of threshold: at what point does the moral cost of compliance exceed the moral cost of rebellion? This is not a question with a clean answer, and anyone who claims otherwise is selling certainty at the expense of truth.

Dissent is not the opposite of loyalty. It is loyalty's most demanding expression — the insistence that a community live up to its own ideals.

The practice of self-examination is essential here. Dissent that has not been examined is merely contrarianism. The obligation to dissent carries with it an obligation to be sure — or as sure as a finite, fallible being can be — that one's dissent is grounded in principle rather than preference, in justice rather than self-interest. This is why the greatest dissenters — Thoreau, Gandhi, King — were also among the most disciplined thinkers of their age.

The question of dissent is inseparable from the question of political legitimacy. If the state's authority rests on ongoing justification rather than original consent, then dissent is not a violation of the social contract but a mechanism for its enforcement. The dissenter says: you have failed to justify this exercise of power. Prove me wrong or change course.

Of course, moral luck plays its part. The dissenter who is vindicated by history is called a hero. The one whose cause fails is called a troublemaker — or worse. The moral quality of the dissent does not change; only its reception does. Rosa Parks and the countless unnamed women who refused to give up their seats before her made the same moral choice. History, in its arbitrary way, remembered one.

In the digital age, dissent has become simultaneously easier and more dangerous. It is easier because the tools of communication are available to everyone. It is more dangerous because surveillance is pervasive, mobs form in minutes, and the line between brave speech and career-ending controversy has never been thinner. The obligation to dissent now requires not just moral courage but a sophisticated understanding of the terrain on which dissent plays out.

We owe it to each other to speak when speaking is hard. Not always. Not about everything. But about the things that matter most — the things our silence would betray.
